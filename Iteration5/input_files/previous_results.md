# Results and Discussion

## 1. Residual GNN Training Performance

The primary objective of the training phase was to optimize a permutation-invariant Interaction Network to learn the residual acceleration $\Delta a_i$ of an N-body system, such that the total predicted acceleration $\hat{a}_i = f_{\text{phys}}(r, v, \epsilon=0.01) + \text{MLP}_{\theta}(r, v)$ accurately captures the Hamiltonian flow. The architecture was intentionally kept lightweight, comprising a total of 1,603 trainable parameters, to ensure rapid convergence within the constrained computational environment. A critical physical prior embedded in the architecture was the strict enforcement of Newton's Third Law. By parameterizing the edge interactions $f_{ij}$ and explicitly enforcing anti-symmetry ($f_{ij} = -f_{ji}$), the network guaranteed that the internal residual forces summed to zero. Numerical gradient checks via finite-difference verification confirmed that the maximum symmetry error was exactly $0.0$, validating the architectural enforcement of momentum conservation.

The training protocol utilized a Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (Neural ODE) framework to learn the continuous vector field from discrete intermediate trajectory snapshots ($t \in \{0.5, 1.0, \dots, 5.0\}$). A pivotal component of this training was the hyperparameter tuning of the energy conservation regularization weight, $\lambda$. The loss function was defined as a composite of the acceleration mean squared error (MSE) and the energy deviation: $\mathcal{L} = \text{MSE}(\hat{a}, a) + \lambda (E_{\text{initial}} - E_{\text{predicted}})^2$. 

During the grid search on the validation set, the impact of $\lambda$ was starkly evident. The unregularized baseline ($\lambda = 0.0$) yielded a validation position MSE of 350.08, but suffered from a catastrophic validation energy MSE of $2.10 \times 10^9$. This indicates that without explicit thermodynamic constraints, the neural network learns a non-conservative vector field that rapidly injects or dissipates energy into the system. In contrast, setting $\lambda = 0.1$ resulted in a lower validation position MSE of 303.94 and reduced the energy MSE to effectively $0.0$ during the grid search phase. At the conclusion of the 30-epoch full training run, the final validation energy MSE was constrained to an impressive $3.72 \times 10^{-10}$. 

We can explicitly conclude that the inclusion of the energy conservation regularization term ($\lambda = 0.1$) significantly improved the physical consistency of the model relative to the unregularized baseline ($\lambda = 0.0$). By penalizing deviations from the initial Hamiltonian state, the regularization forced the network's latent representations to lie closer to the symplectic manifold governing the true dynamics. The model converged rapidly, completing 30 epochs in just 3.52 seconds of wall-clock time, achieving a final training loss of 738.61 and a validation loss of 368.83. However, as subsequent analyses reveal, this short-horizon snapshot fitting did not seamlessly translate to long-term global stability.

## 2. Trajectory Reconstruction Accuracy

While the short-term training metrics demonstrated successful convergence over the $\Delta t = 0.5$ snapshot intervals, the true test of the Neural ODE emulator lies in its ability to reconstruct the full phase-space trajectory from $t=0$ to $T=5.0$ given only the initial conditions. For this evaluation, the trained model was utilized as the derivative function within a leapfrog integration scheme, mirroring the deterministic ground-truth generation process.

The results of the full trajectory rollout on the $N=50$ validation set revealed severe degradation in predictive accuracy. The final validation position MSE at $T=5.0$ exploded to $4.60 \times 10^5$, and the corresponding velocity MSE reached $6.99 \times 10^5$. These massive error magnitudes indicate a fundamental breakdown in the model's ability to emulate the long-term dynamics of the virialized Plummer sphere.

Several factors contribute to this divergence. The gravitational N-body problem is inherently chaotic; the system exhibits sensitive dependence on initial conditions, characterized by positive Lyapunov exponents. Even microscopic errors in the predicted residual acceleration $\Delta a_i$ at early time steps compound exponentially as the integration proceeds. The lightweight nature of the MLP (1,603 parameters) likely lacks the requisite expressivity to capture the highly non-linear, multi-body correlations that arise during complex orbital crossings. While the fixed physical prior $f_{\text{phys}}$ provides a baseline Newtonian force, the network's residual corrections appear to introduce spurious perturbations that destabilize the orbits.

Furthermore, the discrepancy between the training and evaluation integration schemes played a significant role. Due to library availability constraints, the training phase utilized a fallback Runge-Kutta 4 (RK4) solver with internal sub-stepping, whereas the evaluation phase employed a symplectic leapfrog integrator. The neural network effectively learned a vector field optimized for the truncation errors of the RK4 solver. When this learned field was subjected to the leapfrog scheme, the mismatch in numerical integration manifolds exacerbated the trajectory divergence, leading to the observed explosion in MSE.

## 3. Time-Reversibility and Extended Rollout Stability

A defining characteristic of Hamiltonian systems and the symplectic integrators used to simulate them (such as the leapfrog method) is time-reversibility. If a system is integrated forward from $t=0$ to $T=5.0$, negating the velocity vectors and integrating backward for the same duration should perfectly recover the initial state, conserving phase-space volume in accordance with Liouville's theorem.

To evaluate whether the Neural ODE learned a truly symplectic flow, we performed a forward-backward integration test on the test set. The model integrated the initial conditions to $T=5.0$, the velocities were inverted ($v \to -v$), and the system was integrated backward to $t=0$. The reversibility error was quantified by comparing the recovered state to the original initial conditions. The results demonstrated a complete failure of time-reversibility: the reversibility position MSE was $3.25 \times 10^{10}$, and the velocity MSE was $4.58 \times 10^{10}$. 

This catastrophic failure indicates that the learned vector field is highly asymmetric with respect to time reversal. Instead of a conservative Hamiltonian flow, the network has parameterized a dissipative or artificially expansive dynamical system. When the arrow of time is reversed, the spurious non-conservative forces learned by the MLP do not cancel out; rather, they amplify, driving the particles into entirely unphysical regions of phase space.

This instability was further corroborated by the extended rollout test, where the model was tasked with integrating the system up to $T=10.0$ (twice the duration of the training horizon). The extended rollout resulted in a mean relative energy drift of $8.73 \times 10^8$ and a maximum relative energy drift of $2.65 \times 10^{10}$. Despite the strict energy regularization applied during the short-interval training ($\lambda = 0.1$), the model could not maintain thermodynamic stability over long horizons. Once the trajectory drifted slightly off the training manifold, the network encountered out-of-distribution phase-space configurations. Lacking a true understanding of the underlying physics, the model's residual predictions became erratic, injecting massive amounts of artificial kinetic energy into the system and causing the simulated cluster to artificially evaporate or collapse.

## 4. Density-Dependent Error Analysis

In a virialized Plummer sphere, the particle density is highly heterogeneous, featuring a dense central core and a diffuse outer halo. The dynamical timescales and the stiffness of the differential equations vary drastically depending on the local environment. To understand the spatial distribution of the model's predictive failures, we conducted a density-dependent error analysis. Local particle density was estimated for each particle at each snapshot by computing the inverse cubic distance to its 5th nearest neighbor ($1/r_5^3$). The trajectory reconstruction errors were then decomposed into two regimes: the "Core" (representing the top 20% highest local densities) and the "Halo" (representing the bottom 80% lower local densities).

The decomposition revealed a stark contrast in model performance across different physical regimes. For the core particles, the mean position MSE was 435,780.12, and the velocity MSE was 613,969.10. In contrast, the halo particles exhibited significantly lower errors, with a position MSE of 144,812.45 and a velocity MSE of 215,236.48. 

The errors in the dense core are approximately a factor of three larger than those in the sparse halo. This discrepancy is deeply rooted in the physics of the N-body problem. In the core, particles undergo rapid, high-acceleration close encounters. Even with the implementation of a gravitational softening length ($\epsilon = 0.01$) to prevent mathematical singularities, the gradients of the gravitational potential in the core remain exceptionally steep. The dynamics in this regime are highly stiff, requiring precise, high-frequency force calculations to maintain orbital stability. The lightweight Residual GNN struggles to approximate the complex, rapidly fluctuating residual forces required during these close encounters.

Conversely, particles in the halo experience a much smoother, mean-field-like gravitational potential dominated by the collective mass of the core, with relatively few localized two-body interactions. The neural network finds it significantly easier to emulate this smooth, low-frequency dynamic. The density-dependent analysis clearly highlights that the primary bottleneck in the current Neural ODE architecture is its inability to resolve the stiff, high-gradient interactions characteristic of dense stellar environments.

## 5. N-Scaling Generalization

A robust machine learning emulator for physical systems should ideally learn the fundamental laws of interaction, allowing it to generalize to systems of varying sizes and configurations. To test the transferability of the learned dynamics, the model—trained exclusively on systems with $N=50$ particles—was evaluated on newly generated test sets containing $N=25$ and $N=100$ particles. The scale radius ($b=1$) and the total mass normalization were kept consistent, meaning that altering $N$ directly altered the number density and the frequency of pairwise interactions.

The generalization results demonstrated a profound sensitivity to the system size. When evaluated on the smaller $N=25$ system, the model performed relatively well compared to its baseline, achieving a position MSE of 8,177.66, a velocity MSE of 5,779.12, and a mean relative energy drift of 228.09. While these errors are still substantial in an absolute sense, they are orders of magnitude lower than the errors observed on the $N=50$ validation set. In a system with fewer particles, the phase space is less crowded, close encounters are less frequent, and the dynamics are generally less chaotic. The fixed physical prior $f_{\text{phys}}$ dominates the trajectory, and the network's erroneous residual predictions have fewer opportunities to compound destructively.

However, when the model was scaled up to the $N=100$ system, it experienced a catastrophic failure. The position MSE skyrocketed to $4.07 \times 10^{11}$, the velocity MSE reached $2.96 \times 10^{12}$, and the mean relative energy drift exploded to $1.70 \times 10^{10}$. In the $N=100$ system, the particle density is doubled, leading to a non-linear increase in the frequency and intensity of close encounters. The interaction network, having only been exposed to the phase-space density of the $N=50$ system, was entirely unequipped to handle the stiffer, more complex multi-body correlations of the denser system. The message-passing mechanism of the GNN aggregated a higher volume of out-of-distribution edge features, causing the residual acceleration predictions to diverge wildly.

This N-scaling test conclusively demonstrates that the current Residual GNN architecture overfits to the specific macroscopic thermodynamic properties (such as density and relaxation time) of the training set. It fails to learn a universally applicable representation of Newtonian gravity. To achieve true zero-shot generalization across varying $N$, future iterations of the model must likely incorporate scale-equivariant architectural features, higher-capacity MLPs to handle dense message aggregation, and training curricula that expose the network to a diverse range of particle densities and interaction stiffnesses.
