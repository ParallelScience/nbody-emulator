# Symplectic Neural ODE for Virialized N-body Dynamics: Results and Discussion

## 1. Lyapunov Characterization of the N-body System
The gravitational N-body problem is a classically chaotic system, characterized by extreme sensitivity to initial conditions. To establish a theoretical baseline for the predictability of the dataset, we first quantified this chaotic divergence by calculating the global Lyapunov time ($t_\lambda$) of the simulated Plummer spheres. By applying an infinitesimal global perturbation ($\delta r_0 = 10^{-8}$) to the initial phase-space coordinates and tracking the exponential divergence of the trajectories using a high-precision integration scheme, we extracted the maximal Lyapunov exponent for each of the 100 simulations.

The analysis revealed a mean Lyapunov time of $t_\lambda \approx 1.357$ normalized time units. The full distribution of these times across the dataset is visualized in <code>data/lyapunov_histogram_1_20260416_092228.png</code>. This metric is of paramount importance for understanding the fundamental limits of machine learning emulation in this domain. The total integration time for the dataset is $T = 5.0$, which equates to approximately $3.68$ Lyapunov times ($T / t_\lambda \approx 3.68$). In chaotic dynamical systems, the phase-space volume stretches exponentially over time. Consequently, attempting to learn a direct, static mapping from the initial state at $t=0$ to the final state at $t=5.0$ requires the neural network to implicitly model this exponentially diverging flow. Such a task is fundamentally ill-posed for standard regression models, as infinitesimally close initial conditions in the training distribution map to macroscopically distinct final states, leading to severe overfitting or regression to the mean. This theoretical constraint necessitates the shift from direct state prediction to learning the continuous local vector field (accelerations) via a Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (Neural ODE) framework.

## 2. Acceleration Learning and Ablation Findings
To model the underlying vector field $\dot{z} = f(z, \theta)$, we implemented a permutation-invariant Graph Neural Network (Interaction Network). This architecture inherently respects the symmetries of the N-body problem, treating particles as nodes and their pairwise interactions as edges. Furthermore, the network was constrained to strictly obey Newton's Third Law by anti-symmetrizing the edge outputs ($f_{ij} = -f_{ji}$), ensuring global momentum conservation by design.

The model was trained to minimize the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of the predicted accelerations across 10 intermediate trajectory snapshots. As depicted in <code>data/training_curve_1776331914.png</code>, the training converged smoothly, achieving a final training MSE of $0.1008$ and a validation MSE of $0.1340$. The absence of significant divergence between training and validation losses indicates that the GNN's inductive biases effectively prevent overfitting, even within the relatively small dataset of 100 simulations.

A critical component of this research was the ablation study evaluating the necessity of physical priors in learning gravitational dynamics. We compared two variants of the GNN: a "Physics-Informed" model, which received the analytical softened gravitational force ($f_{\text{phys}}$ with $\epsilon=0.01$) as an explicit input feature to the edge multi-layer perceptron (MLP), and a "Learned-Only" model, which relied exclusively on raw kinematic features (relative positions and velocities).

The results, illustrated in <code>data/ablation_mse_bar_1_1776332435.png</code>, demonstrate a catastrophic failure of the Learned-Only approach. The Physics-Informed model achieved a test-set acceleration MSE of $0.1574$, whereas the Learned-Only model yielded an MSE of $243.2167$—a degradation of over three orders of magnitude. This stark contrast highlights a known limitation of standard neural networks: spectral bias. MLPs struggle to approximate functions with steep gradients and singularities, such as the inverse-square law ($1/r^2$) of Newtonian gravity. During close encounters, the forces scale rapidly, and without the analytical prior, the network fails to capture the asymptotic behavior of the potential well. By injecting the analytical force, the Physics-Informed GNN is relegated to acting as a residual learner. It does not need to discover the $1/r^2$ law from scratch; instead, it learns to refine the dynamics, correct for discretization errors, and smooth the integration manifold, resulting in highly accurate acceleration predictions.

## 3. Hamiltonian Consistency and Energy Drift
While accurate acceleration prediction is necessary, it is not sufficient for stable long-term emulation. The N-body system is a Hamiltonian system, meaning its true vector field is conservative and preserves phase-space volume (Liouville's theorem). To evaluate whether the GNN learned a physically consistent Hamiltonian flow, we integrated the learned force field over the full time horizon ($T=5.0$) using two distinct numerical schemes: the symplectic Leapfrog integrator and the non-symplectic 4th-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) integrator.

We quantified the physical fidelity of the rollouts by measuring the "Energy Drift," defined as the variance of the normalized total energy $\text{Var}(E/E_0)$ over the trajectory. The results, detailed in <code>data/energy_drift_analysis_1_1776332826.png</code>, are highly revealing. When integrating the GNN with the Leapfrog scheme, the energy drift was $0.1354$. However, when using the higher-order RK4 scheme, the drift increased substantially to $0.8579$.

This discrepancy is a profound indicator of the model's learned structure. RK4 is a highly accurate integrator for general differential equations, but it is not symplectic; it artificially dissipates or injects energy over time because it does not preserve the symplectic 2-form of Hamiltonian mechanics. The Leapfrog integrator, conversely, exactly conserves a "shadow Hamiltonian" that remains close to the true energy of the system. The fact that the GNN + Leapfrog combination significantly outperforms GNN + RK4 implies that the neural network has successfully internalized a pseudo-Hamiltonian vector field. The learned forces are sufficiently conservative and curl-free that applying a symplectic integrator yields the expected physical benefits.

We also compared these results against a baseline MLP force field, which maps the entire global state to global accelerations. The MLP exhibited lower energy drifts ($0.0346$ for Leapfrog and $0.0345$ for RK4). However, this metric is deceptive. Global MLPs frequently fail to capture the high-frequency, localized dynamics of close particle encounters, instead learning an overly smoothed, mean-field approximation of the forces. This results in "frozen" or highly damped trajectories where particles barely interact, artificially keeping the total energy constant because the kinetic energy remains stagnant. The GNN, by explicitly modeling pairwise interactions, captures the true dynamic range of the virialized Plummer sphere, resulting in a more realistic, albeit slightly more variable, energy profile.

## 4. N-Scaling Generalization
A primary advantage of utilizing Graph Neural Networks is their inherent size-invariance, allowing a model trained on a specific number of particles to be evaluated on systems of arbitrary sizes. We conducted a zero-shot generalization test by evaluating the GNN (trained exclusively on $N=50$ particles) on newly generated test sets of $N=25$ and $N=100$ particles, initialized from the same Plummer sphere distribution.

The quantitative results averaged over 10 test simulations per configuration are as follows:
* **$N=25$:** Acceleration MSE = $0.0577$, Energy Drift = $1.7994$
* **$N=50$:** Acceleration MSE = $0.1574$, Energy Drift = $0.2844$
* **$N=100$:** Acceleration MSE = $0.4476$, Energy Drift = $0.1547$

The scaling of the Acceleration MSE is consistent with the physical properties of the Plummer sphere. As $N$ increases within a fixed scale radius ($b=1$), the central density of the system increases, leading to a higher frequency of close encounters and larger aggregate forces. Consequently, any fractional error in the GNN's pairwise force prediction is amplified by the denser environment, resulting in a higher absolute MSE for $N=100$. Conversely, the $N=25$ system is sparser, the forces are weaker, and the absolute MSE is naturally lower.

However, the Energy Drift exhibits an inverse and highly counter-intuitive scaling: energy conservation improves as the number of particles increases. The $N=25$ system suffers from severe energy drift ($1.7994$), while the $N=100$ system achieves a drift of $0.1547$, outperforming even the $N=50$ training distribution. This phenomenon can be explained through the lens of statistical mechanics and the mean-field limit. In sparse systems ($N=25$), the global gravitational potential is highly granular and dominated by discrete, chaotic two-body interactions. A single erroneous force prediction by the GNN during a close encounter can drastically alter the kinetic energy of the involved particles, leading to macroscopic energy non-conservation. As $N$ grows towards the mean-field limit ($N=100$), the global potential becomes smoother and is dictated by the collective mass distribution rather than individual encounters. The GNN's small, zero-mean errors in pairwise force predictions effectively average out over the larger number of edges (a manifestation of the Law of Large Numbers). This error cancellation leads to a more stable global potential and, consequently, superior global energy conservation, demonstrating that the GNN captures the macroscopic thermodynamics of the system even better than its microscopic kinematics.

## 5. GNN vs. MLP Benchmark Comparison
To synthesize the findings and validate the core hypothesis of this research, we benchmarked the Neural ODE approach (GNN integrated over time) against a Direct MLP baseline. The Direct MLP was trained to map the initial phase-space configuration $\mathbf{x}(t=0)$ directly to the final state $\mathbf{x}(t=5.0)$, bypassing intermediate dynamics entirely.

The results, summarized in the composite figure <code>data/benchmark_results.png</code>, definitively prove the inadequacy of direct temporal mapping for chaotic systems. The Direct MLP achieved a catastrophic MSE of $732.8916$ at $t=5.0$. As established in Section 1, the integration window spans nearly 3.7 Lyapunov times. Over this duration, the phase space undergoes extreme stretching and folding. Two initial conditions that are nearly identical will diverge exponentially, ending up in entirely different regions of the phase space. The Direct MLP, which is mathematically constrained to learn smooth, continuous functions, cannot represent this highly discontinuous mapping without an exponentially large parameter space and an impossibly dense training dataset. Faced with this chaotic divergence, the MLP likely collapses to predicting the spatial mean of the dataset (e.g., placing all particles near the center of mass with near-zero velocity), resulting in massive positional and velocity errors.

In stark contrast, the Symplectic Neural ODE approach decomposes the intractable global prediction problem into a tractable local learning problem. By utilizing a Physics-Informed GNN to learn the instantaneous, deterministic vector field, the model operates independently of the Lyapunov time. The chaotic divergence is handled not by the neural network's weights, but by the numerical Leapfrog integrator, which traverses the learned vector field step-by-step. This separation of concerns—leveraging machine learning for local physical approximations and robust numerical methods for global temporal integration—is essential. The results confirm that while neural networks cannot easily learn to predict the future of a chaotic N-body system directly, they can successfully learn the rules that govern it, enabling accurate, energy-conserving, and scalable emulation.
